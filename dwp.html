Universal Credit – terminal illness

I worked with a User Researcher and Content Designer to help improve a section of the Universal Credit registration process which dealt with a question around terminal illness.

The current DWP policy deems anyone with less than 6 months to live as being terminally ill and is therefore fast tracked into a group that are paid at a higher rate and aren’t required to look for work.

If a claimant selected that they have a terminal illness during registration they would then receive a callback to receive additional support.

Almost all of these callbacks (to claimants) were not deemed to be terminally ill.

Firstly, we needed to understand why so many claimants were incorrectly stating that they were terminally ill.

Secondly, we wanted to improve the experience for people who had a terminal illness.

Insights

  • We spoke with internal subject matter experts and colleagues in Manchester who were tackling a similar issue with the health registration questions.
  • It became clear that the DWP policy definition of terminal illness (6 months or less) was in stark contrast to the user’s understanding.
  • People can have a terminal illness and still be expected to live beyond 6 months (for example: motor neurone disease).

Prototype V1

We decided to dig a bit deeper into the terminal illness comprehension and were able to meet with 5 users at the PIP assessment centre in Canterbury and show them our initial prototype.

Prototype version 1

As expected we found the majority of the users didn’t entirely understand what terminal illness is.

All of the users we spoke with had long term health conditions (asthma, diabetes etc) and some thought of these as terminal, and therefore indicated that they would select terminal illness.

Prototype V2

For the 2nd iteration we removed the first screen and dropped the term terminal illness from the question. We introduced less than 6 months to live to match with the DWP terminal illness policy.

Prototype version 2

This more direct line of questioning worked better with users who had a long term illness.

We added the callback screens and a line of text on the final screen which read, your phone might show our number as blocked, or say ‘no caller ID’.

This was to try to reduce the problem agents were having with claimants not answering their phones.

Final round

We took the prototype to the Maggie’s centre (cancer support) in Fulham where we sat down with some of their support staff to walk them through our research and prototype.

Here we gained further knowledge around the struggle that terminally ill patients (or their carer) tend to face when trying to claim benefits.

Impact

The redesigned journey was added to the Universal Credit registration flow and helped reduce the incorrect callback rate by 78%.

This saved the department a substantial amount of money whilst creating an improved experience for claimants.
